About Crowdinvesting

I have heard about crowdfunding, but what is crowdinvesting?

As you may know, crowdfunding provides the opportunity for real estate operators to raise funds by soliciting investment opportunities to a group of investors. Rather than buying the entire property, investors avoid the nuances of everyday property ownership, while still benefiting from the financial upside potential of real estate investing. Crowdinvesting is a similar strategy to crowdfunding, but considered to be the more “investor-focused” aspect of the crowdfunding space. While crowdfunding is tailored to the fundraiser, crowdinvesting is specifically tailored to you, the investor.

Why is this not more popular?

Crowdfunding has been used for generations and is a very familiar concept in Europe; however, it has yet to fully catch on in the US. The idea of “investment crowdfunding” was proposed in 2012 when Title II of the JOBS Act and proposed modifications to preexisting legal framework in the Securities Act of 1933. These changes were formalized and became operative in 2013, when the SEC lifted a century-long ban as to how companies can "generally" solicit and raise capital for projects from accredited investors. This relax in regulation has paved the way for a variety of crowdsourcing sites across almost every industry

How can Peloton Street help me?

By engaging in crowd-investing, you can diversify your portfolio by purchasing shares in more than one direct real estate investment opportunities with less money than what would be typically required to close a single deal - all, without the headache of managing the property, its finances, and other day-to-day affairs.

What is an accredited investor or qualified purchaser?

An accredited investor is an individual that has made $200,000 for the past two years or who combined with a spouse has made $300,000 per year and has an expected income in the current year of at least these levels. Alternatively, an individual can qualify as an accredited investor if you have a net worth of $1 million or more, excluding your primary residence. See here for a complete definition of an accredited investor.

The definition for a qualified purchaser is little more complicated, but generally most people visiting our site will fall into one of the following groups:

  1. Individuals who own $5 million in investments, which include securities, financial contracts entered into for investment purposes, cash, cash equivalents held for investment purposes, real estate held for investment purposes, CDs, bankers acceptances and other similar bank instruments held for investment purposes. Investments do not include real estate held for personal purposes, jewelry, art, antiques, and other collectibles. Debt used to acquire the investments is excluded from the value of the investments;
  2. Institutional investors who own $25 million in investments; and
  3. A family owned company that owns $5 million in investments; and
  4. Trusts with less than $25 million, where the trustee and each person who contributes assets to the trust is a Qualified Purchaser.

The Investment Process

When I invest in equity investments on Peloton Street, what do I own?

Our investment opportunities are structured into a series of Special Purpose Vehicles ("SPV") such as a Limited Liability Company ("LLC") in which our investors are can purchase shares in individual properties. Investor’s capital is pooled into a single purpose LLC and that LLC provides a single investment into the opportunity. An LLC provides limited liability protection for our investors, while streamlining the reporting and distribution processes. This is a fairly common strategy in the world of real estate investing and most crowdfunding companies are taking this same approach.

One advantage to investing with Peloton Street is that we specifically structure the deals as partnerships with real estate operators in which our founding team serves as principals for each transaction (not as an agent)this provides you, as the investors, with someone to look after your best interest from investment acquisition to disposition.

Who makes decisions in an LLC?

Each LLC is governed by a document called an “operating agreement”. While every operating agreement is slightly different, our deal are typically structured as a partnership between the real estate operator and the founders of Peloton Street who serve as the manager and limited members (investors). The managers represent a team of highly skilled asset managers who are assigned to monitor the investment's performance and look after your best interest. We are highly experienced in this capacity and since we have a “carried interest” in each deal, we make money when you make money.

We believe this to be an optimal structure for our members providing you with a passive real estate investment and an alignment of interests between us as the manager and you as the limited member.

Legal, Regulatory, and Technical

How does the JOBS Act impact Peloton Street?

The JOBS Act was signed into law on April 5, 2012 by President Obama; Title II of the JOBS Act became effective on Sept. 23, 2013 and Title III is under an SEC rule making review. Title II of the JOBS Act,which modified the preexisting legal framework known as “Regulation D” in the Securities Act of 1933, enables private companies like Peloton Street to "generally solicit" accredited investors using social media, Internet and traditional advertising. Eventually, Title III of the JOBS Act will become operative and enable investment crowdfunding for all investors, both accredited and non-accredited. We look forward to watching this rule making process continue and working to understand how it will impact our business model.

What are the major regulations that apply to companies like Peloton Street?

There is a lot of "buzz" around crowdfunding and how it is impacting or will impact real estate investing. Honestly speaking, the industry as a whole is so new that the "jury is still out" trying to determine a verdict; however, companies in this space are currently only able to solicit “accredited investors” or "qualified purchasers" by relying on how Title II of the JOBS Act has modified the preexisting legal framework known as “Regulation D” in the Securities Act of 1933. Regulation D Rule 506(c), which became operative in September 2013, is new and allows a private company to solicit accredited investors using social media, Internet and traditional advertising. There’s no limit on how much an accredited investors can commit to deals under this structure. Title III of the JOBS Act, which is still under SEC rule making review, has proposed adding a new section to the Securities Act that will allow for "true crowdfunding" of transactions by allowing non-accredited investors to participate. There’s also Regulation A and the pending Regulation A+. Regulation A allows an issuer to raise $5mover a rolling 12month period from"local investors," without having to file a registration with the SEC and, in some cases, individual state regulators. These investors must be domiciled in and the adjacent states where the transaction is located or issuer is based. Regulation A is considered a low threshold with a lot of effort, which is why the SEC and state regulators are considering Regulation A+, where issuers will be able to raise $50 million but with greater investment protections. Finally, there’s an S-1, which is the strategy used by Lending Club used and is similar to an IPO.

Are we regulated by the SEC, FINRA, and/or separate branch of the government? How is Peloton Street legally structured?

You betcha! Peloton Street has taken a very conservative approach to its regulatory strategy, in an effort to provide deal makers and investors with the safest and best-in-class crowdinvesting platform available today. We are not only structured as a "private equity fund" relying on exemptions under the Investment Company Act of 1940 that will issue its own exempt securities under Regulation D Rule 506(b) and 506(c), but also the co-founders of Peloton Street are registered representatives of a broker-dealer in good standing with the SEC, FINRA, and member of the SIPC.

Our affiliation with a broker-dealer will help Peloton Street govern the entire solicitation process, compliance, and provide a legal framework for us to be able to collect certain fees and reimbursements for deal expenses. More strategically, we appreciate the robust regulatory framework that is already in place by the SEC and FINRA for broker-dealers and the economic scale that can be achieved by creating such a best-in-class platform. Many of our competitors believe that the only way to run a "truly compliant shop" is to be associated with a broker-dealer - and we agree. Peloton Street subscribes to the fact that real estate investors will (and should) feel more comfortable in investing with a broker-dealer and this will eventually become a prerequisite for Venture Capital investors looking at crowdfunding platforms. Furthermore, Peloton Street will initially also be able to rely on the "private fund exemption" in the Investment Adviser Act of 1940 until it has $150 million assets (i.e. investments) under management.
